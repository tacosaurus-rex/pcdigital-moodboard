# pdp-ecommerce notes

Product detail pages worth stealing from. Each reference lists its captures, the
one-line reaction at capture time, and a short read-between-the-lines analysis of

## Zero Acre Farms, Cultured Oil

Source: https://shop.zeroacre.com/products/zero-acre-cultured-oil
Captured: 2026-05-27

Captures in this set:
- `01-shop-zeroacre-com.png` (full page)
- `02-shop-zeroacre-com-ingredients-popup.png` (buy-box "Ingredients & Nutrition Facts" popover, open)

Reaction: I love the charts, I love the value props on the product images, I love the
branding cohesion, I love the information when you click the links in the buy box and
the way it pops up.

Why this works:
- The comparison charts turn abstract health claims into scannable visual proof. The
  "checks every box" table positions Zero Acre against unnamed competitors and lets a
  skimmer grasp the differentiator (monounsaturated fat, smoke point, sustainability)
  in seconds, with almost no reading.
- Value props baked onto the product imagery mean the benefit travels with the product,
  so it still lands when the image is seen out of context (social, ads, a quick scroll).
- Branding cohesion is itself a trust signal. One restrained palette (sage, cream,
  forest green), one script logotype, a tight type scale. For an unfamiliar ingredient
  like cultured oil, "looks like a premium food brand, not a supplement" does a lot of
  the persuasion before any copy is read.
- The buy-box popover is progressive disclosure at the decision point. Full nutrition is
  one click away exactly where you decide to buy, with no page navigation and no losing
  your place. It serves the detail-hungry buyer without cluttering the page for everyone
  else.

Steal this: pair every health claim with a visual proof unit, keep the palette ruthlessly
tight, and put progressive-disclosure popovers at the buy box rather than on a separate
specs page.

## Buoy, Hydration Drops (unflavoured flagship)

Source: https://justaddbuoy.com/products/hydration-drops
Captured: 2026-05-27

Captures in this set:
- `03-justaddbuoy-com.png` (full page)
- `04-justaddbuoy-com-supplement-facts.png` (Supplement Facts drawer, open)
- `05-justaddbuoy-com-clinical-study.png` (clinical trial drawer, open)
- `06-justaddbuoy-com-third-party-testing.png` (third-party testing drawer, open)

Reaction: I love the design of the page, I like the UGC section, I love the modal that
pops up with more information and the detailed information they provide, I love the gifs.

Why this works:
- The drawers are the whole strategy. Heavy, credibility-building content (a University
  of Pittsburgh placebo-controlled clinical trial, third-party testing for microplastics
  and heavy metals, the full supplement facts) is tucked behind taps so the main page
  stays light and conversion-focused. Sell on emotion and simplicity up top, then satisfy
  the rigorous buyer in the drawers. That is the core move.
- Even the due-diligence content is designed, not dumped. Supplement Facts is an icon
  checklist plus photography plus a dietician note, the clinical drawer uses stat rings
  (64% and 49% more hydrating). Regulatory and proof content gets the same brand care as
  the hero, so doing your homework still feels on-brand.
- The UGC and review volume (11,000+ reviews) provide trust at scale, and lifestyle
  imagery adds aspiration alongside the proof.
- The gifs demonstrate the product ritual (drops dissolving, "add to any beverage") in a
  way static images cannot. For an unflavoured additive, motion makes an invisible benefit
  feel tangible and satisfying.
- The positioning is confident and ownable: bright lime on teal, and "unflavoured" run as
  the hero benefit rather than apologised for. The whole page commits to the contrarian angle.

Steal this: keep the PDP emotionally light and fast, but build a proof layer in drawers
(clinical, testing, full facts) for the skeptic, design that proof layer with full brand
care, use short looping gifs to show the product ritual, and let UGC carry trust.

## Foreign Waters, Energy Flow (paraxanthine energy drink)

Source: https://foreignwaters.com/
Captured: 2026-05-28

Captures in this set:
- `07-foreignwaters-com.png` (full page)
- `08-foreignwaters-com-hero.webm` (hero slider playback)
- `08-foreignwaters-com-hero-01.png` through `-06.png` (six frames of the slider cycle:
  silhouetted surfer at sunset, underwater turquoise wave, silhouette drinking from the
  bottle, plus transitions)

Reaction: Not the most sophisticated overall, but the slider where the main hero image
usually sits is cool, mixing video and statics that slide through. For a one-colour
brand, the white plus yellow accent is clean and sophisticated. White reads scientific
and healthy, the yellow keeps it warm. The soft rounded edges make it less harsh.

Why this works:
- The hero is the move. Where most PDPs put a hero product shot, Foreign Waters runs a
  cinematic slider of lifestyle and nature footage (sunset surf, ocean texture, silhouetted
  figures drinking) mixing video and stills. The headline "Own your energy. Own your life."
  stays put while the imagery cycles, so the copy persists and the visuals do the emotional
  lifting. You are sold on a feeling (aspiration, vitality, the elements) before you ever
  see the can. Mixing video and statics is the practical trick: video where it earns the
  weight (waves splashing, the drink ritual), photography for the in-between beats.
- One-colour discipline carries the brand. The whole page is essentially white with black
  type and a single warm-yellow accent (top promo bar, price chip, marquee underline,
  newsletter CTA). For a category dominated by neon and edgy black, white reads clinical,
  healthy, scientific. That is exactly the positioning a paraxanthine product wants, and
  the one yellow note keeps it from feeling sterile or pharma.
- Rounded edges humanise the clinical. The "Shop now" button is a full pill, product image
  tiles use generous corner radius, modals round their corners. Take those away and the
  same white-on-white palette would feel like a lab. With them it reads like a premium
  minimalist food brand. This is the cheapest single move on a stark interface.
- Motion at two cadences. Below the hero slider, a testimonial marquee scrolls under it
  ("GAME CHANGER!", "MY COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE", "CLEAR HEAD, CALM NERVES") at a different
  speed. The eye always has something to track at the top of the page without anything
  shouting.
- The image grid below the hero acts like a moodboard inside the PDP. Square tiles of
  product close-ups and lifestyle shots in the same white-and-yellow world, so you absorb
  the brand voice as a vibe before you read a single claim.

Steal this: replace the static product hero with a slider mixing one or two short videos
and a couple of statics, and keep the headline as overlaid DOM text so the words stay put
while imagery cycles. For a science-led product default to white as the dominant non-colour
and pick exactly one warm accent. Use generous corner radius on every primary control and
card to take the edge off. Layer two pieces of subtle motion at different cadences rather
than one big motion piece.

## Patterns across these PDPs

Two distinct moves to steal:

1. **Progressive disclosure of proof** (Zero Acre, Buoy). For science-led or unfamiliar
   products, keep the first impression clean and confident, then make the rigorous evidence
   (full nutrition, clinical trials, third-party testing) one tap away in a popover or
   drawer. Serves the skeptic without cluttering conversion for everyone else.
2. **Cinematic emotional hero plus restraint elsewhere** (Foreign Waters). Replace the
   static product hero with a slider mixing video and stills so the page sells aspiration
   first, product second. Pair with brand restraint elsewhere (one accent colour, generous
   radius, lots of whitespace) so the hero stays the punch.

Cross-cutting: all three look like premium consumer brands, not supplements. The clinical
or technical content is dressed up in brand craft, never surrendered to regulatory
blandness. That is the bar for our own PDPs.
